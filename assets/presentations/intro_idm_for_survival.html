Infectious Disease Modelling

An introduction for survival analysts

Kylie Ainslie

2026-06-16

What is infectious disease modelling?

The quantitative study of how infectious diseases move through populations.

Mathematical and statistical models of:

  • states an individual can be in — susceptible, infectious, recovered, …
  • transitions between those states
  • rates at which those transitions happen, and how to infer them from data

The unit of analysis is a population, not a single patient.

● Susceptible: 199 ● Infectious: 1 ● Recovered: 0

What can IDM help us answer?

  1. Describe — What is the epidemic doing right now? How fast is it growing? How many people have been infected?

  2. Predict — What will happen next? When will the outbreak peak? How many hospitalisations next month?

  3. Decide — What should we do about it? Will school closures slow transmission? How many people need to be vaccinated to stop spread?

All three require a model of how infection moves through a population.

Let’s build one from scratch

We want to describe what happens to people during an epidemic.

Step 1: What states can a person be in?

  • Susceptible — not yet infected, could become infected
  • Infectious — currently infected and able to transmit
  • Recovered — immune, no longer at risk

Step 2: How do people move between them?

The SIR model

Two transitions. Two rates. Each rate is a hazard.

Infectious disease modelling

The SIR model is a multistate model.

But there is one critical difference

In a survival study

The hazard \(h(t)\) is determined by:

  • the patient’s age, treatment, covariates
  • external factors

It does not depend on what other patients are doing.

In an epidemic

The hazard — the force of infection \(\lambda(t)\) — is:

\[\lambda(t) = \beta \cdot \frac{I(t)}{N}\]

It depends on how many people are infectious right now.

Your risk today is created by the epidemic itself.

The hazard is endogenous. It rises and falls with \(I(t)\).

What does β · I(t)/N mean?

\[\lambda(t) = \underbrace{\beta}_{\text{transmission rate}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{I(t)}{N}}_{\text{probability your contact is infectious}}\]

  • \(\beta\) = (contacts per day) × (probability of transmission per contact)
  • \(I(t)/N\) = fraction of the population that is currently infectious
  • \(\lambda(t)\) = the hazard of infection on day \(t\)

As \(I(t)\) grows, the hazard grows. As \(I(t)\) falls, the hazard falls. This feedback loop is what makes epidemic dynamics different.

The SIR equations

\[\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta \frac{I}{N} \cdot S\]

Susceptibles are depleted as they become infected

\[\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta \frac{SI}{N} - \gamma I\]

Infectious cases arrive via transmission, leave via recovery at rate γ

\[\frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I\]

Recovered grow as infectious cases clear; mean infectious period = 1/γ days

We can write the system as a series of differential equations which are very similar to a survival model if we assume an exponential hazard. That is one of the key features of a compartmental model is that they assume exponentially distributed inter-event times. Which might be a limiting assumption.

What an epidemic looks like

sirData = {
  let S = 0.999, I = 0.001, R = 0;
  const beta = 0.4, gamma = 0.15, dt = 0.5;
  const rows = [];
  for (let t = 0; t <= 120; t = Math.round((t + dt) * 10) / 10) {
    rows.push({ t, S, I, R });
    const dS = -beta * S * I * dt;
    const dI = (beta * S * I - gamma * I) * dt;
    S += dS; I += dI; R -= dS + dI;
  }
  return rows;
}

The epidemic rises, peaks, and falls — all driven by the depletion of susceptibles.

How fast does it spread?

Basic reproduction number \(R_0\)

How many people does one infectious person infect, on average, before they recover?

  • If \(R_0 > 1\) → each case generates more than one new case → epidemic grows
  • If \(R_0 < 1\) → epidemic fades out
  • If \(R_0 = 1\) → endemic equilibrium

R₀ in the SIR model

  • One infectious person is in state \(I\) for an average of \(1/\gamma\) days
  • During that time they make contact at rate \(\beta\)
  • So on average they infect \(\beta \times 1/\gamma\) others in a fully susceptible population

\[R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}\]

A ratio of two survival quantities: the transmission rate and the recovery rate.

For the epidemic above: \(R_0 = 0.4 / 0.15 \approx 2.7\). Each case generates ~2–3 secondary cases.

As the epidemic unfolds: Rₜ

\(R_0\) assumes a fully susceptible population. As susceptibles are depleted, the effective reproduction number \(R_t\) declines:

\[R_t = R_0 \cdot \frac{S(t)}{N}\]

The epidemic turns when \(R_t\) crosses 1 — the point at which \(S(t)/N = 1/R_0\).

Crossing the threshold

Herd immunity threshold: the fraction immune needed to push \(R_t\) permanently below 1:

\[p_c = 1 - \frac{1}{R_0}\]

For \(R_0 = 2.7\): \(p_c \approx 63\%\).

Once 63% of the population is immune — through infection or vaccination — each case generates fewer than one new case on average and the epidemic fades.

Controlling spread: interventions and vaccination

Two ways to push \(R_t\) below 1:

Reduce susceptibles — shrink S

Vaccination moves individuals out of S before exposure. In the model, vaccinated individuals transition to a protected state V.

\[R_t = R_0 \cdot \frac{S(t)}{N} \downarrow \text{ because } S \downarrow\]

VE determines how completely vaccination removes individuals from the susceptible pool.

Reduce transmission — lower β

Non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs): masks, distancing, school and workplace closures.

\[R_t \downarrow \text{ because } \beta \downarrow\]

The transmission rate drops; the shape of \(R_t\) over time reflects when interventions are imposed and lifted.

Both levers work by changing the pace of spread, which is determined by the generation interval.

The generation interval

The generation interval (GI) is the time between infection of an infector and infection of their infectee.

The generation interval

  • Each infected person generates \(R_t\) new cases on average.
  • Cases appear with a delay determined by the generation interval \(w(\tau)\).

Summing contributions from all past cases gives the renewal equation:

\[i(t) = R_t \int_0^\infty i(t{-}\tau)\, w(\tau)\, d\tau\]

  • \(w(\tau)\): probability density of the time from primary to secondary infection
  • Short \(w(\tau)\) → rapid generations → harder to contain

The figure shows a simulated epidemic with 22 days of incidence data.

Top panel: daily case counts (bars) with the generation interval distribution w(τ) overlaid (purple curve, scaled to the bar heights). The red dashed line marks the current day t. The coloured bars are the cases that fall within the GI window — they are the ones that can plausibly have generated today’s cases. The colour gradient (yellow → red) reflects how much each past day contributes: days near the peak of w(τ) contribute most.

Bottom panel: Rt estimated from the renewal equation at each day. Rt starts well above 1 (epidemic growing), crosses 1 near day 18 (the red dot — the epidemic is at its peak), and falls below 1 as it declines.

The key point: Rt is not observed directly. It is inferred by comparing today’s incidence to the weighted sum of past incidence, where the weights come from w(τ). The shape of w(τ) — how quickly or slowly the GI distribution decays — determines how far back you need to look and how sensitive the Rt estimate is to recent case counts.

Serial interval vs generation interval

The serial interval (SI) is the time between symptom onset in an infector–infectee pair.

\[\text{SI} = \text{GI} + (\text{infectee incubation} - \text{infector incubation})\]

SI and GI are similar on average, but differ for individual pairs — and SI can even be negative when the infectee develops symptoms before the infector.

The connection to survival analysis

The hazard function, renamed

Survival analysis

\[h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Pr(t \le T < t+\Delta t \mid T \ge t)}{\Delta t}\]

\[S(t) = \exp\!\left\{-\int_0^t h(u)\, du\right\}\]

\(S(t)\) = probability of not yet having the event

Infectious disease modelling

\[\lambda(t) = \beta \cdot \frac{I(t)}{N}\]

\[S(t) = \exp\!\left\{-\int_0^t \lambda(u)\, du\right\}\]

\(S(t)\) = fraction of the population still susceptible

Same equation. \(S(t)\) is simultaneously a survival curve and the susceptible fraction.

The left column shows the standard SA setup. The hazard h(t) is the instantaneous rate of the event given that it has not yet occurred — the probability of the event in the next instant, conditional on survival to t. Integrating it gives the cumulative hazard H(t), and exponentiating gives S(t).

The right column shows the SIR model. The force of infection λ(t) = β · I(t)/N is the instantaneous rate at which a susceptible individual becomes infected — exactly the hazard of infection. Integrating it and exponentiating gives S(t), the fraction still susceptible.

These are the same mathematical object. A susceptible person in the SIR model is following a survival process with hazard λ(t). The only structural difference — which we covered earlier — is that λ(t) is endogenous: it rises and falls with the epidemic itself, whereas in a standard SA model the hazard is determined by fixed individual characteristics and external covariates.

This is the deepest connection between the two fields. Everything else in this section builds on it.

Real epidemics are messier

SIR is a starting point. Real outbreaks introduce complications that don’t fit neatly into standard SA frameworks:

Latency

People are infected but not yet infectious — a hidden state between S and I.

Standard SA: add a sojourn distribution. But the latent state is unobserved.

Multiple outcomes

Infection can lead to recovery, hospitalisation, or death — sometimes all three in sequence.

Multi-state SA handles event chains well. The challenge: in an epidemic, transition rates depend on the current number infectious — and individual event times are often unobserved, only aggregates are reported.

Heterogeneity

Not everyone is equally susceptible, infectious, or at risk. High-risk individuals are infected first, changing the composition of the remaining susceptible pool over time.

Standard SA: frailty models help, but don’t capture epidemic feedback.

IDM’s response: bespoke compartmental models

You are not limited to SIR. You define whatever compartments the disease biology requires:

But bespoke models are harder

The flexibility comes at a cost:

  • More parameters: each transition rate needs to be estimated; many are correlated
  • Latent states are unobserved: E, H, V compartments are not directly measured; inference relies on imperfect surveillance data
  • Identifiability problems: with aggregate data, you often cannot distinguish between competing explanations (e.g. waning immunity vs susceptible depletion)
  • Computational burden: likelihood evaluation requires solving ODEs or simulating the IBM at every parameter value
  • No standard software: no single tool covers the full modelling pipeline; models are typically coded from scratch

When compartmental models aren’t enough: IBMs

Some questions require more structure than any compartmental model can provide:

  • Household transmission: who infects whom within a household depends on who lives together
  • Contact networks: workplaces, schools, and communities create heterogeneous mixing
  • Individual exposure histories: individuals may experience numerous exposures: previously infected, then vaccinated, then re-infected, and how that sequence shapes their outcome

Individual-based models (IBMs) simulate each person separately, with their own attributes and contact events. The epidemic emerges from millions of individual interactions rather than from differential equations.

The case study uses HERMES, an IBM with household, school, workplace, and community transmission — which is why the output is a dataset of individual-level infection and event times rather than aggregate curves.

This is also why SA tools apply directly: the data structure IBMs produce is exactly what SA is designed to analyse.

This is where survival analysis comes in

The data challenges

The surveillance gap

Epidemic surveillance systematically fails to capture what we want to observe.

What we want What we get SA analog
When did each case occur? Report date — days or weeks after onset Right-truncation
When was each person infected? Exposure window + symptom onset window Doubly interval-censored
How many infections occurred? Confirmed cases — a fraction of the true total Missing data
Causal effect of vaccination? Observed rates in confounded observational data Confounded time-varying HR
Which deaths were caused by the epidemic? Total deaths — cause unattributed Competing risks

Reporting delays Right-truncation

The gap: cases occurred earlier than the data suggests — recent events are systematically missing because reporting takes time.

The fix — nowcasting — is a deconvolution of the reporting-delay distribution.

Incubation period Doubly interval-censored

The gap: infection time is unobserved — we only know a window of possible exposure \([E_L, E_R]\) and a window of possible onset \([O_L, O_R]\).

The incubation period \(T = O - E\) is doubly interval-censored. EM, Bayesian augmentation, and multiple imputation all apply.

Getting this right matters: for diseases where transmission peaks before symptoms, the most infectious moment has already passed by the time onset is observed.

Underreporting and serology Missing data

The gap: most infections are never confirmed — reported cases are a fraction of the true total. Serological surveys recover the missing signal: seroprevalence \(\pi(t) = 1 - S(t)\).

Vaccine effectiveness Confounded time-varying HR

The gap: \(VE(t) = 1 - HR(t)\), but vaccination is not randomised — high-risk unvaccinated individuals are infected first, making the comparator group progressively lower-risk and creating the illusion of waning.

Excess mortality Competing risks

The gap: we know deaths occurred, but not which were caused by the epidemic. Excess mortality — observed minus expected — is the competing-risks problem with an unobserved cause indicator.
